The Problem of Food Labels

For most people a trip to the grocery store might not be much of a task. Your shopping list might be simple listing the items for todays dinner, and as soon as you find the items you need you are ready to go. However, many people who live with disabilities, mental and/or physical, that require them to pay extra attention to what the products they buy contain. Some might suffer from allergies to specific ingredients while others might have special dietary needs due to sickness or disability.

Reseach have found that those who are suffering from mental disabilities often also have problems related to nutrition and need to be extra attentive of what they eat on a daily basis [1]. For them going through a package description might prove difficulties due to cognitive impairment. In general, small print on packages combined with excessive information makes it a difficult and time consuming task to skim through it all. We aim to solve this problem and make grocery shopping easy for everyone.

Our Target Group

Our solution is aimed at those who need to pay extra attention to their food intake and nutrition due to their disability, including potential caretakers. The following examples illustrate some different disabilities which relates to the need of dietary awareness.

Example 1:
Downs Syndrome

Downs Syndrome is a genetic condition where they are born with an extra chromosome which affects their physical and mental development [2] . Many people suffering from Downs Syndrome also suffer from obesity. This can be due to hypothyroidism or due to inactivity which is an effect of their syndrome [3]. They also are more often prone to coeliac disease compared to the rest of the population [4]. Some people with Downs syndrome are however able to live on their own and care for themselves, but due to a weaker sense of judgement this often contributes to more weight gain [1] . Another issue which is important to keep in mind, is that reading- and number ability is often lower in people with Downs Syndrome [5]. From this we can understand that a person with Downs Syndrome has more challenges when it comes to nutrition and in understanding package labeling.

Example 2:
Type 2 Diabetes

Type 2 diabetes can be a genetically inherited or a lifestyle condition. Usually caused by inactivity and a sugar rich diet. This causes weight gain which again causes insufficient production of insulin by the pancreas [6]. Type 2 diabetes can be regulated and treated with a healthy diet as well as a strict regulation of sugar intake. By regulating the amount of simple carbohydrates such as pasta and food with high sugar content which lead to glucose spikes, and replacing them with complex carbohydrate which can be found in vegetables and lead to a regular glucose levels, stabilizing the HbA1c levels long-term, thus preventing nervedamage. [7]. This leads to a significant reduction of chance for later complications like nerve damage or loss of sight. Additionally it reduces the risk of developing cardiovascular problems and also reduced the overall chance of contracting severe chronic infection of wounds. Avoiding excessive amounts of sugar and saturated fat, while increasing the intake of unsaturated fat can help improve the condition [6] . We can imagine that someone diagnosed with age-related diabetes will have to alter their eating habits drastically. They will have to read countless of labels to figure out what they can eat and how much they can eat of it.

Example 3:
Food Allergies

Food allergies are quite common and can occur in many different ways. Some might be allergic to large amounts of certain foods while they can still eat small amounts of it. Others can get severe allergic reactions to even the smallest exposure. Allergies might change over the years. For example, allergies to milk is common among small children but they usually outgrow the allergy in time [8]. The only efficient way to treat food allergy is by avoiding the food ones reacts to [9]. We can imagine a family with a small child who has been diagnosed with lactose intolerance, an allergy of milk and/or dairy products. The family’s diet and shopping habits will have to be drastically altered in order to accommodate the child's needs. Milk is part of many products which are not dairy products like pre-made meatballs, in liver pate, or anything made with butter. In other words, it’s not enough for the family to just avoid the obvious dairy products, but will have to keep aware of the labels of their purchases.

Our Solution

Our solution is FoodWiz, an app which lets you scan the barcode on your groceries when you are out shopping. The app will give you the specific information you are seeking which makes it easier for you to find a suitable product. The app also has adjustable settings to set according to your dietary needs.

How it Works

You start by opening the app which leads you directly to the set-up menu for first time users, from there you can set your preferences in accordance with your needs. After putting in the needed data you’re ready to go. After the initial set up it will directly go to scan mode when you open the app.

In settings you can alter how the app displays it’s feedback. For someone with a food allergy it will be sufficient to know if the products contains the ingredient they are avoiding or not. In this case a red cross will appear on the screen along with a short vibration to signalize that the products contains the ingredient. If the ingredient is not there it will display a green circle without vibration.

For someone who need to know the specific amount of e.g carbohydrates and how much of those are sugars, the app will display the specific numbers and information they need. The information is displayed with simple symbols which are easy to understand even for someone who does not understand the meaning of the text.

In all these cases you are able to tap on the information sign for more detailed information as well as suggestions to similar products more suitable to your needs.

Our app FoodWiz is made with the cooperation of the largest food store chains in Norway, such as Tine, Orcla Foods and so on. With the approved data of these major companies the app can accurately display the needed data for the intended user. This way we secure accurate and reliable information which is continuously updated as products are altered.

Accessibility

As this app is targeting people with various disabilities, we want to focus on a simple and easy to understand design.

We have included features such as text to speech for those with difficulties to see or read. Through the use of simple icons along with the feedback we are including those with mental disabilities who might have trouble understanding what the numbers really mean. From our example above we can image that it might be difficult to understand what it means if a product contains “350kcal pr 100gr” for someone with Downs Syndrome. In the apps setting the person can set personal limit values in cooperation with their physician. This way a warning triangle might appear if the value exceeds a certain amount, while a red cross appears if it exceeds the maximum limit.
